# The move library

Each move: what to watch for, why it works, how to run it, and a worked example.
The examples are drawn from a real grilling session with a mortgage team owner;
generalize the pattern, not the industry.

## 0. Calibrate the mode

Before any move lands, know how this user metabolizes challenge. Two modes, same
inversion underneath:

- **Socratic:** the user wants to be interrogated toward the insight. Turns end on
  one or two pointed questions. The user does the discovering; you catch what's
  wrong with their answers. Tells: "grill me," "poke holes," "challenge me,"
  reflective and expansive replies.
- **Directive:** the user wants the failure modes named and a path forward built
  from their inputs. Turns end on findings and a concrete call to action; commands
  that function as questions ("Name the manager or pull the foot-traffic numbers")
  are legitimate closers, and a turn may wrap the whole loop. Tells: "just tell me,"
  "what should I do," terse or action-seeking replies, visible impatience with
  question volleys.

If unclear, ask once at the start. Recalibrate mid-session — a Socratic opener can
earn a directive close once the mechanism is on the table, and a directive user who
starts reflecting has just invited deeper questions. In both modes, sequence the
arithmetic: locate the core personal mechanism first, then bring the ledger to size
and confirm it (or assign the number-pull as homework).

## 1. Reject comfortable inversions

**Watch for:** answers to "what would guarantee failure?" that can't indict the
answerer.

Two species:

- **The act of God.** "It fails if the market collapses / a recession hits / the
  business dries up." True, uncontrollable, and therefore useless. The user picked a
  failure mode they don't own so the answer couldn't implicate them.
- **The sermon.** "It fails if I stop doing the thing I already believe in" (stop
  tending relationships, stop working hard, get complacent). Also true, and also
  comfortable, because the remedy is "keep being me."

**How to run it:** name the dodge out loud, then re-ask with a constraint: "Try
again. You're only allowed mechanisms you control." Or sharpen the target: "What is
[person] carrying right now that you would refuse to carry yourself?"

**Worked example:** asked what would guarantee his key employee quits, the owner
said "if the business dried up." Re-asked with the constraint, the real answer
surfaced: the employee was alone on a line that just lost a colleague to friction,
carrying the load, with his bonus *shrinking* because of the owner's own hiring
mistake. The act of God was hiding a mechanism the owner was building himself.

**Sub-tell — loyalty as evidence:** "He's always stuck through the hard times" is
what everyone says the week before the loyal one resigns. Loyalty is not capacity.
The workhorse doesn't complain; he breaks all at once.

## 2. Blame is not a control

**Watch for:** "this is 100% on me," full ownership, self-flagellation.

**Why it matters:** ownership is the *start* of the fix, not the fix. The surgeon
who feels terrible still needs the checklist. Feelings don't fire under pressure;
systems do. And desperation — the exact condition that caused the mistake — is the
condition under which any mental-only rule will be skipped.

**How to run it:** credit the honesty specifically (honest autopsies are rare), then
ask: "What is the *written* screen the next decision goes through, no matter who
brings it to you? If it lives in your head, it doesn't exist."

**Worked example:** owner took full blame for a bad hire sourced from a respected
friend's referral during a desperate quarter. Credited, then converted: a one-page
hiring screen, disqualifiers first, with an explicit rule that the screen runs
*regardless of who referred* — the more the owner likes the referrer, the more
mandatory the checklist, because liking is precisely the solvent that dissolved it
last time.

## 3. Invert the inverter (name the tendencies)

**Watch for:** any surfaced mistake. The question is never just "what went wrong"
but "what in *you* produced it."

**The usual suspects:** incentive-caused bias (hunger makes a man see food
everywhere), liking, authority, social proof, consistency/commitment, sunk cost,
availability (recent/vivid beats relevant), deprival superreaction (a promised thing
withheld sours faster than a thing never promised).

**Why it matters:** stacked tendencies are a lollapalooza, and from the inside a
lollapalooza feels like *conviction*. The user wasn't deciding anymore; they were
rationalizing. If they can't name the tendencies, they'll repeat the error with
better vocabulary.

**Worked example:** the bad hire = liking (friend's referral) + authority (respected
source) + incentive-caused bias (desperate for production). Three tendencies, one
direction. The owner described it himself as "I stacked a bunch of bullshit on top
of each other" — that's a lollapalooza in the vernacular.

## 4. The iron rule of incentives

**Watch for:** comp plans, bonus pools, promised promotions, partner arrangements —
anything where you can ask "what behavior does this *actually* reward?"

**Hunt these perverse cases:**

- **Taxed loyalty:** the person who stays absorbs the workload of the person who
  left, while the shared bonus pool *shrinks* because of the departure and its
  cleanup costs. Reward for staying: more work, less money.
- **Unwritten promises:** comp formulas and promotion conditions that exist only as
  conversations. Unwritten generosity motivates nobody; unwritten promises curdle.
  Fix: on paper, this week, with numbers and a rough date.
- **Promises gated on the promiser's weakness:** "You'll be promoted when I hire
  people for you to manage," said by someone who has never systematically hired.
  The promisee's future is hostage to the promiser's weakest skill.

**How to run it:** trace the mechanism forward from the *other* person's chair,
today. "Run it from her side: what happened to her workload this month, and what
happened to her payout?"

## 5. Detection tests or it's a wish

**Watch for:** the user producing a good list of disqualifiers (bad hires, bad
clients, bad partners) with no way to detect any of them before committing.

**Why it matters:** every disqualifier worth having is invisible in an interview.
Blame-shifters arrive with hero stories. Loafers say "team player" most. Whiners are
charming for ninety minutes; it's ninety days they can't fake. A list without tests
is a list of things the user will discover in month four, again.

**Test patterns that work:**

- **Serial autopsies:** "Walk me through your last three deals/projects that died."
  Count self-attribution. Three autopsies, zero ownership = disqualified. People
  rehearse hero stories, not honest post-mortems.
- **Provenance:** where did their last twelve months of results actually come from?
  Verifiable names and sources. Then verify with the sources, not the boss.
- **Downward references:** never reference-check only upward. Call the people who
  worked *under or beside* them. One question: "Would you work with them again?"
  The pause is the answer.
- **Live scenario:** don't count years of tenure, test the hands. One genuinely
  hairy real-world scenario, walked live, twenty minutes. Tenure says where the
  chair was, not what the hands did.
- **The bluff catch:** include something the candidate *can't* know and watch
  whether they ask or improvise. Interview bluffing predicts live bluffing.

**The tripwire:** before day one of any commitment, write what must be observed by
day 90 and what triggers the exit. Pre-committed criteria fire when in-the-moment
judgment won't, because in the moment, consistency tendency and sunk cost will
rationalize. ("I probably knew very quickly" + the hire lasting months = the proof.)

## 6. Data over memory

**Watch for:** the user describing their business from recall. The recall is stale.
Availability bias means their attention map tracks who *was* important.

**How to run it:** get the actual records — reports, exports, screenshots — and
diff them against the narrative. Specifically:

- **The stale roster:** ask who their top sources/clients are, then check. Expect
  to find someone significant the user never mentions. ("You never said his name
  once, and he's your top referrer this half.")
- **Filters that hide the funnel:** minimum-threshold filters (≥2 deals, ≥$X)
  exclude precisely the new relationships that are the future. Today's top sources
  all started below the filter line.
- **Whole-portfolio moves:** one source going cold is about the source. *Everything*
  cooling at once means the common factor is the market or the user. Check the
  user's calendar for the confounding variable — what else consumed their attention
  that period?
- **Tachometer vs odometer:** the gut tracks activity (calls, warmth, pipeline);
  the ledger tracks results (closings, revenue). Both are real instruments. Don't
  let the user read the tachometer and call it the odometer.
- **The recovery move:** when data threatens the narrative, the user will first
  hunt the data error that protects the most *flattering* finding. Allow the
  correction — sometimes they're right, and if they come back with "I was wrong,"
  celebrate it, that's the Darwin discipline. Either way, then ask: "Does the fix
  change the *unflattering* findings, or just the comfortable one?"

## 7. Find the hidden bridge

**Watch for:** structural facts mentioned in passing. "There are only four lenders
in the program and two of them are us." "We're the only vendor certified in the
county." The user often owns a toll bridge they've been treating as a club
membership.

**How to run it:** when a moat appears, invert it both ways. Defense: what takes the
bridge away (who controls the cap, the one active competitor outworking a distracted
owner, the inactive competitor's seat getting filled by someone hungry, the seat
being personal rather than institutional). Offense: what flow is crossing the bridge
unharvested, and which assets the user *already owns* could be bolted together to
feed it. When several owned assets point the same direction, name the favorable
lollapalooza explicitly — it's rare and worth betting big on.

**Screen the opportunity too:** don't let the user sprint indiscriminately at the
newly discovered upside. Rank the targets, run the same disqualifier screen used
for hires, and pursue the few that pass. Indiscriminateness is the desperate-hire
error in an opportunity costume.

## 8. Sequence before opening taps

**Watch for:** enthusiasm to launch a campaign, channel, or expansion while the
delivery machinery is wounded.

**Why it matters:** new relationships are won or lost on the first delivery. Botch
deal one with a new partner and there is no deal two. If the back office is one
loyal person carrying a departed colleague's load, opening a new tap converts
opportunity into reputation damage.

**How to run it:** "Before you open this tap: can the current line deliver a
flawless first impression N times over? If not, what's the order of operations?"
Usually: stabilize the team (written comp, filled seat) → then the campaign. Slow
is fine. Fumbling the courtship because the back office buckled is the expensive
kind of irony.

## Closing a session

Assign homework in numbers and names: which figures to pull, which calls to make
(name the people), which documents to draft, by when. If artifacts were produced,
list their open items. If the user keeps a decision log, ask which of today's
conclusions graduates into it. End the way you started: one sentence of credit,
earned, not cheap.
