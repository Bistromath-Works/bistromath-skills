# General Presentation Frameworks

## Overview

This reference provides proven presentation frameworks for various presentation types and purposes. Use these as starting structures, adapting to specific content and audience needs.

## Framework Selection Guide

| Presentation Type | Best Framework | Key Focus |
|------------------|----------------|-----------|
| Teaching/Training | Explain-Demo-Practice | Comprehension & retention |
| Persuasive/Pitch | Problem-Solution-Benefit | Decision-making |
| Technical Deep Dive | Context-Concept-Application | Understanding |
| Conference/Keynote | Hook-Journey-Insight | Engagement & inspiration |
| Status Update | Progress-Challenges-Next | Clarity & alignment |
| Strategic Planning | Situation-Options-Recommendation | Decision support |

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Problem-Solution-Benefit (Persuasive)

**Best for:** Sales pitches, proposals, change initiatives

**Structure:**
1. **Problem** (25% of presentation)
   - Make the pain tangible and specific
   - Quantify the impact
   - Show why now is the time to solve it

2. **Solution** (40% of presentation)
   - Introduce your approach
   - Explain how it works
   - Show proof (demos, case studies)
   - Address obvious objections

3. **Benefit** (25% of presentation)
   - Specific outcomes and ROI
   - Timeline and implementation
   - Risk mitigation
   - Next steps

4. **Call to Action** (10% of presentation)
   - Clear, specific ask
   - Easy path forward

**Slide Allocation Example (20 slides):**
- Opening: 1 slide
- Problem: 4-5 slides
- Solution: 8-9 slides
- Benefit: 4-5 slides
- CTA: 1 slide

---

### 2. Explain-Demo-Practice (Educational)

**Best for:** Training sessions, workshops, skill development

**Structure:**
1. **Context & Objectives** (10%)
   - Why this matters
   - What they'll learn
   - How it fits into their work

2. **Explain** (30%)
   - Core concepts
   - Key principles
   - Common misconceptions to avoid

3. **Demonstrate** (30%)
   - Walk through real examples
   - Show step-by-step process
   - Highlight tips and tricks

4. **Practice** (20%)
   - Hands-on exercises
   - Guided application
   - Common mistakes to watch for

5. **Resources & Next Steps** (10%)
   - Where to get help
   - Additional learning materials
   - How to continue practicing

**Key Principle:** Multiple micro-demos throughout, not one big demo

---

### 3. What-So What-Now What (Informative)

**Best for:** Updates, reports, information sharing

**Structure:**
1. **What** (30%)
   - Facts, data, findings
   - What happened or what exists
   - Context and background

2. **So What** (40%)
   - Why it matters
   - Implications and insights
   - What it means for the audience
   - Patterns and trends

3. **Now What** (30%)
   - Recommendations
   - Decisions needed
   - Action items
   - Next steps

---

### 4. Situation-Complication-Resolution (Strategic)

**Best for:** Business strategy, complex decision-making

**Structure:**
1. **Situation** (20%)
   - Current state
   - How we got here
   - Relevant context

2. **Complication** (30%)
   - What's changed or what's challenging
   - Why the status quo won't work
   - Risks and consequences of inaction

3. **Resolution** (40%)
   - Recommended approach
   - Why this solution fits
   - Implementation plan
   - Expected outcomes

4. **Decision Point** (10%)
   - What needs to be decided
   - Options (if applicable)
   - Timeline for decision

---

### 5. Hook-Journey-Insight (Inspirational)

**Best for:** Keynotes, conference talks, thought leadership

**Structure:**
1. **Hook** (15%)
   - Surprising fact or story
   - Provocative question
   - Bold statement
   - Personal anecdote
   - Goal: Grab attention immediately

2. **Journey** (60%)
   - Build narrative arc
   - Share discoveries or transformation
   - Include setbacks and learnings
   - Make it personal and relatable
   - Weave in key insights

3. **Insight** (20%)
   - Core message or principle
   - Broader implications
   - Challenge to audience
   - Memorable takeaway

4. **Call to Reflection/Action** (5%)
   - What they should think about
   - What they can apply
   - How to continue the conversation

**Key Principle:** Story-driven, not slide-driven

## Advanced Frameworks

### The Hero's Journey (Transformation)

**Best for:** Case studies, transformation stories, change narratives

**Simplified Structure:**
1. **Ordinary World** - Where we/they started
2. **Call to Adventure** - The catalyst for change
3. **Challenges & Tests** - Obstacles encountered
4. **Transformation** - How things changed
5. **Return with Gift** - What was learned and can be shared

### The Pyramid Principle (McKinsey)

**Best for:** Executive presentations, consulting-style decks

**Structure:**
- **Top Line** (1 slide): Main recommendation/conclusion
- **Supporting Arguments** (3 slides): Key reasons why
- **Evidence** (6-9 slides): Data and analysis backing each argument

**Principle:** Start with the answer, then support it (not the other way around)

### The Monroe Motivated Sequence (Persuasive)

**Best for:** Motivational talks, advocacy

**Structure:**
1. **Attention** - Grab interest with surprising fact
2. **Need** - Establish the problem that must be solved
3. **Satisfaction** - Present the solution
4. **Visualization** - Help them see the future with solution
5. **Action** - Clear call to action

## Presentation Flow Principles

### Opening Strategies

**The Surprise**
- Start with unexpected data or fact
- "Did you know...?"
- Challenges assumptions

**The Question**
- Rhetorical: "What if...?"
- Direct: "How many of you...?"
- Creates curiosity

**The Story**
- Personal anecdote
- Customer story
- Relatable scenario

**The Bold Statement**
- Provocative claim
- Clear position
- Demands attention

**Avoid:**
- Lengthy introductions
- Agenda slides (unless necessary)
- Apologies or caveats

### Transition Techniques

**Signposting:**
- "Now that we've seen the problem..."
- "Let's turn to how this works..."
- "Here's what this means for you..."

**Bridge Questions:**
- "So what does this tell us?"
- "Why does this matter?"
- "How do we apply this?"

**Visual Transitions:**
- Section divider slides
- Progress indicators
- Recurring visual themes

### Closing Strategies

**The Summary**
- Recap key points (rule of 3)
- Reinforce main message
- Connect back to opening

**The Challenge**
- Call to action
- Call to reflection
- Inspire change

**The Vision**
- Paint picture of future
- Show what's possible
- End on optimistic note

**Avoid:**
- "Any questions?" as your last words
- Fizzling out
- Introducing new information
- "Thank you" slides (just say thanks)

## Slide Structure Best Practices

### Slide Types

**Title Slides**
- Clear, descriptive headlines
- Communicate the point, not just the topic
- ✅ "AI reduces loan processing time by 60%"
- ❌ "Loan Processing"

**Content Slides**
- One idea per slide
- Headline states the conclusion
- Body provides supporting evidence
- Visuals > text when possible

**Transition Slides**
- Signal section changes
- Minimal text
- Visual continuity

**Data Slides**
- Clear labels and legends
- Highlight key insights
- Tell what to look at
- Avoid chart junk

### The 1-6-6 Rule

- **1 idea** per slide
- **6 bullets** maximum
- **6 words** per bullet (roughly)

Exception: High-level audiences may prefer more text in "read-ahead" decks

### Visual Hierarchy

**Use size and color to show importance:**
- Main point: Large, bold
- Supporting detail: Smaller, regular weight
- Meta information: Smallest, gray

**White space is your friend:**
- Don't fill every pixel
- Let content breathe
- Guide the eye with emptiness

## Adapting to Audience

### For Executive Audiences

- Lead with recommendations
- Heavy on "So what," light on "What"
- Fewer slides, more insight
- Include backup slides for questions
- Be prepared to skip/reorder on the fly

### For Technical Audiences

- Include methodology and details
- Show your work
- Address edge cases
- Provide resources for deeper learning
- Welcome interruptions and questions

### For Mixed Audiences

- Layer information (headline for all, details for some)
- Use progressive disclosure
- Create clear slide sections for different audiences
- Provide handout with full details

### For Large Groups

- Bigger text and visuals
- High contrast colors
- Less interaction, more polling/rhetorical questions
- Record for those who can't see/hear well

### For Small Groups

- More interactive
- More flexibility in structure
- Can be more conversational
- Adapt in real-time to feedback
