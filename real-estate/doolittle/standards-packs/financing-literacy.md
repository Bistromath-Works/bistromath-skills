# Standards Pack: Financing Literacy (v1)

Authority: Eirik Rorvig — 16 years in finance and
lending. Extracted 2026-07-09. This pack is the sole source of truth for coaching this domain.

## §1 The standard

**The bar:** *the connector operating at full power — fluent enough to re-explain, humble enough
not to originate.*

**First commandment:** better no advice than bad advice.

The five judgment habits that separate top-decile agents:

1. **They know they are not financiers.** They hold the direction (the asset is the goal), never
   the specificity (that belongs to the money people).
2. **They hear money as a multi-step process.** Acquire the asset at imperfect terms; restructure
   the terms later. The asset can't be bought later at today's price; the terms can be.
3. **They verify instead of repeat.** An agency verdict outranks what another agent heard in
   passing. "Other people have done it" is not evidence.
4. **They ask before they answer.** Great ones interrogate the scenario with genuine curiosity;
   average ones assume their way to the easiest path *for themselves*.
5. **They route by acuity.** Flu → clinic; appendix → surgeon. They know which seat owns which
   question, and that some questions belong to none of the three seats.

## §2 Doctrines (what "fluent enough to re-explain" means)

### D-1 · The asset-over-terms model — and its boundaries
Buying at a higher rate/loan amount is acceptable because terms are restructurable and the asset
is the goal. **Wrong when:** (a) large down payment on a 15-year-or-shorter note — terms
dominate; (b) rate-cycle bottoms (2020–21) — the terms *are* the once-in-a-generation asset.
The agent may hold this model as direction; the numbers behind it are always the lender's.

### D-2 · The mortgage lifecycle: acquire for cash flow, restructure for wealth
The two halves below are **stages, not competing rules** — acquisition first, restructuring at
the review points (D-5). Unifying frame: **optimize where each dollar works hardest — never the
rate sticker.**

**Stage 1 — Acquisition: a lower rate is a feels thing, not a logic thing.** Least amount down
the first time; the property appreciates on the bank's money while the client's capital keeps
compounding elsewhere. When the down payment would come from an invested asset, the question is:
*what's your rate of return on that asset?* Pulling money earning ~10% to buy a rate down trades
future gains for short-term comfort. In the current market (expensive houses, high rates,
cash-flow-driven buyers), the 30-year fixed is the acquisition tool — especially for first-time
buyers — and that's fine, because of stage 2.

**Stage 2 — Restructuring: you win on term, not rate.** This principle is aimed at people
poo-pooing a 30-year's high rate — it's a *refinance* principle. At the 3–5 year reviews, if
affordable, cut years off and accelerate the payoff, **unless** the client is earning an
outsized return on investments, in which case stay invested and at most drop the rate. Which way
the dollar goes is exactly the plan the financial advisor owns (D-6).

**The verified math (for the agent's own conviction, not for client recitation):** $400K loan.
30-yr @ 6%: by month 180, **$315,871.76 paid in interest, $284,195.38 still owed**. 15-yr @ 8%:
**$288,069.50 total interest, balance zero, done** — less interest than the 30-yr accrues in the
same 15 years, despite the higher rate. Full-term: $463K vs $288K. Riders: the 15 never needs a
refinance; the 30's realistic playbook (hold 5–7 yrs, refi at ~$5K a time, usually rolled into
principal) quietly grows itself.

**The client-facing script (say this, nothing fancier):** *"A 30-year fixed is great for cash
flow — in the business we call it a cash-flow mortgage. It's not meant to be the long-term tool
for your financial growth and wealth. At some point you'll want to reduce the term and
accelerate the payments — unless you're getting a great rate of return on an investment, in
which case keep the money there and maybe just drop the rate. To come up with the actual plan,
talk to your financial advisor — and then let's execute that plan for you."*
Best case, the agent doesn't run this speech at all and simply refers. But if they talk, they
say **"cash-flow mortgage"** — never "compound interest," never amortization mechanics. The
script routes to the advisor *first* (architect before builder), and delivering it earns the
agent real cachet: the client hears someone guarding their wealth, not chasing a commission.

### D-4 · Equity events: the trigger map
People don't know they can use equity to do other things. The agent's job: hear the trigger, ask
the follow-up, route.

| Client says | Mechanism to hear |
|---|---|
| "I have a house to sell after we close" (**the #1 trigger**) | Proceeds need a plan → "What are you planning to do with the proceeds?" |
| "Big down payment, but we're taking on debt to do it" | Cash-out refinance later can retire that debt |
| "We're paying cash" | Delayed financing — refinance right out, recover up to 80% |
| "I'll sell, then refinance my payment lower" | Recast — lump-sum paydown + re-amortize (~hundreds of $, not a ~$5K refi); refi later if rates warrant |
| "I don't want to pay mortgage insurance again" | Proceeds → pay down → remove MI → recast |
| Investor selling a rental | 1031 — always route, never explain |

### D-5 · The 3–5 year review
Plant at closing, live cadence after. Every 3–5 years: what's changed in your life, are you
hitting your financial goals, do you need the lender or the planner? Backbone: adult life
restructures roughly every 5–7 years (Levinson), and the average mortgage lives only ~5–7 years
while people now stay ~11–12 — the restructuring happens with or without you. Sales principle
101, not just finance.

### D-6 · The routing map
**Planner = architect** (intent, depth-and-breadth, the executable plan). **Lender = builder**
(execution; needs the plan first). **Agent = connector** ("the guy who knows a guy"; owns finding
the right house and winning it). Some questions leave all three seats (LLC/tax → attorney/CPA).
Two-layer rule: a question can be *conceptually* the planner's and *literally* the lender's
("should I pay off student loans before buying?" — intent vs qualification mechanics).

Reference routing (from §1 authority): 15-vs-30 → lender/planner · affordability → lender/planner
(depends on post-transaction goals) · sell-or-rent the old house → planner (intent) · time the
market / wait for rates → lender · gift funds → lender · neighborhood as investment → agent ·
401(k) for down payment → planner (never agent) · waive inspection → strictly agent · entity
structuring → attorney/CPA.

**The handoff script (status-preserving):** "I'm an expert at real estate — finding your home and
getting you in position to get it. What I'm not is an expert at lending, financial planning, or
insurance. But I know amazing people in those categories, and I want to be your guy who knows the
guy. Do you have somebody, or can I connect you with my people?"

**The stubborn client:** don't argue the fee; reframe by acuity (clinic vs surgeon — "I'm a
financial surgeon; I do one thing all day, every day"). Stubbornness is emotional, not logical:
control-seekers get rope; smarter-than-you types get to feel smart while you sit on your hands
and let reality grade the DIY plan.

### D-7 · Calm is a relay, not a temperament
The exemplar rep: nervous first-time buyers, agent stays calm *because he verified with the
lender first*, then relays: "I've talked to him, it's handled, here's where it stands."
**Sequence: verify → reassure → absorb panic, never amplify it.** Reassuring blind is a promise
you can't keep, in a comforting voice. Clients are scared because they don't understand the
nuance, don't want to, are nervous by nature, or are first-timers whose whole life is changing.

**The cadence principle:** problem-first versus emotion-first is a *cadence*, not a capability —
and calm, problem-first leadership produces smoother transactions. But never skip the emotion to
get to the problem; that escalates people. The fix is Chris Voss's tactical empathy: **label the
emotion calmly ("it sounds like this feels enormous right now"), then move to the problem.**
Acknowledge without amplifying. (The job is "30% skill, 60% therapist.")

## §3 Failure modes (each is a drill scene)

1. **The warrantable condo.** New-build condo, legal phase incomplete. Fannie AND Freddie say
   not warrantable. Another agent's counter: "other people have financed these conventional."
   Agent repeats it; buyer lands at a non-QM lender a month later, **$9,000 in points** poorer.
   Lesson: the evidence hierarchy — verify, don't repeat.
2. **"These borrowers will be easy."** The #1 promise-lie told to lenders. Lesson: you cannot
   know that; what would you have to ask first?
3. **"My lender can get it done, no problem"** — promised without asking the lender one question.
   Lesson: never spend someone else's capacity.
4. **The double contingency lie.** List agent promises a 3-week sale; buyer's agent writes
   non-contingent on the strength of it against the lender's explicit instruction. Two
   commissions dressed as strategy. Lesson: whose interest does this plan serve?
5. **The Devin pattern.** Told "not qualified, we need time" → writes the offer anyway → expects
   coal-to-diamond. Lesson: the lender's no is information, not an opening bid.
6. **Last-deal poisoning.** "My last deal worked this way." Guidelines change; every file is a
   new file.

## §5 Drills

Format: play the counterpart (client, lender, other agent). **Always withhold one critical fact
that surfaces only if the agent asks.** Scenario skins are regenerated each rep; lessons repeat
until passed.

- **D1 · Diagnostic (first run):** First-time buyers, payment anxiety ("this rate feels too
  high, maybe we should wait"). Hidden facts: down payment is coming out of a brokerage account
  returning ~9%; they also have a condo to sell after closing. Pass requires surfacing both and
  routing (lender + "what happens to the proceeds?").
- **D2 · Trigger ear:** casual conversation about school districts; client drops one D-4 trigger
  sentence mid-stream. Grade: caught it / follow-up asked / routed, not prescribed.
- **D3 · Evidence hierarchy:** listing agent asserts hearsay ("buyers have been getting
  conventional on these"). Hidden fact: it's a non-warrantable new-build. Pass = "who verified
  that, and with whom?" before relaying anything to the buyer.
- **D4 · The promise trap:** lender-facing rep. Agent must describe a new buyer to the lender
  WITHOUT promising ("easy borrowers, quick close"). Pass = facts and questions only.
- **D5 · The handoff:** client asks a planner question (401k, sell-or-rent, student loans).
  Grade delivery of the routing script — status-preserving, connects to a named person, no
  freelance answer first.
- **D6 · The 8pm panic call:** scared buyer, deal mid-underwriting hiccup. Hidden fact: agent
  hasn't talked to the lender yet today. Pass = verify first, label the emotion, relay facts,
  no blind reassurance.
- **D7 · The stubborn DIYer:** client refuses the planner referral, "I can figure it out."
  Grade: acuity reframe (clinic/surgeon), no arguing, no capitulation into giving the advice
  themselves.
- **D8 · The cash-flow mortgage script:** client fixated on rate ("6.8% is robbery, we should
  wait"). Agent must deliver the D-2 client-facing script in their own words — "cash-flow
  mortgage," lifecycle framing, route to the advisor first. Pass = fluent, stayed on direction,
  zero amortization mechanics, zero prescribed numbers, handoff landed. Fail = quoted math at
  the client or freelanced past the script.

## §6 Rubric (every rep, every time)

| Axis | Pass looks like |
|---|---|
| Questions before answers | Interrogated the scenario before responding; found the withheld fact |
| Verified vs repeated | Distinguished evidence classes; checked with the authority before relaying |
| Routed vs originated | Held direction, handed specificity to the right seat, script intact |
| Calm as relay | Verified before reassuring; labeled emotion, then moved to the problem |
| Promise discipline | Zero commitments made on someone else's capacity or timeline |

Grade: **pass / partial / fail**, one sentence naming the correction. Corrections get written to
the curriculum file — a correction that isn't logged doesn't exist.
